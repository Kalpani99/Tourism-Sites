Adisham Bungalow

Adisham Bungalow is a colonial-era mansion located in Haputale, Sri Lanka. It was originally built in the early 20th century by Sir Thomas Villiers, a British planter and a former Chief Justice of Ceylon. The bungalow served as his country residence and a plantation retreat in the scenic hill country. The architecture of Adisham Bungalow reflects the Tudor style, with red brick walls, timbered gables, and beautiful gardens surrounding the property. The bungalow was constructed using materials imported from England, giving it a distinctive European charm. In 1961, the property was acquired by the Roman Catholic Church and converted into a monastery. The Cistercian monks, originating from the Mount St. Bernard Abbey in England, transformed Adisham into a tranquil monastery and a place of religious devotion.Today, Adisham Bungalow serves as a popular tourist attraction and a religious site. Visitors can explore the picturesque surroundings, admire the well-maintained gardens, and visit the chapel inside the bungalow. The monastery's peaceful atmosphere and stunning panoramic views of the tea estates and valleys make it a serene retreat for both locals and tourists seeking solace in the midst of nature.
